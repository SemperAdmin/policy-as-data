How a policy issuance is turned into structured data: the two layers, the shared identifier spine, the rules the schema enforces, and how it all stays consistent.

The scheme separates what a document says from where its authority comes from, and joins them with one addressing system. It follows the House's USLM approach to publishing statute as XML, extended downward through the federal hierarchy to DoD and the Marine Corps.

Layer 1 — the document-structure schema (XSD)

A document is an <issuance> containing <meta> and a recursive tree of <paragraph>s.

<issuance identifier="…" issuanceType="MARADMIN" status="VERIFIED">
  <meta> originator, dtg|date, signer?, subject, derivesFrom* </meta>
  <paragraph identifier="…" status="VERIFIED" level="…">
    <num style="…">8</num>
    <heading>…</heading>
    <content><p>…</p></content>
    <paragraph>…</paragraph>   <!-- nests to any depth -->
  </paragraph>
</issuance>

The schema makes the discipline non-optional. It enforces four invariants:

  1. Everything is addressable. @identifier is required on the issuance and every paragraph.
  2. Verification is on the face of the data. @status (VERIFIED / UNVERIFIED) is required everywhere — you cannot encode a provision without declaring whether it is confirmed against the source.
  3. Numbering is data, not formatting. Paragraphs nest to any depth, and <num style="…"> names the designator's kind (arabic, alpha-lower, paren-arabic, paren-alpha, paren-alpha-upper, paren-roman-lower/-upper, compound). This is what makes 8.a.(1)(b) and the statute's (h)(1)(B)(i)(I) exact, addressable structure.
  4. It generalizes across document types. <num> is optional (named memo sections), <meta> takes dtg or date+signer, and @level labels the structural unit (section/attachment/ enclosure/chapter/appendix/ part). One schema validated a naval message, a DoD memo, a Marine Corps Order, a DoD instruction, and a U.S. Code section.

Layer 2 — the authority ontology (OWL / RDF)

This is the part beyond USLM: a small ontology in the pol: namespace defining a ranked hierarchy of authority and the relationships between issuances.

The hierarchy (lower level outranks higher)

1  Statute, PublicLaw
2  CodeOfFederalRegulations, ExecutiveOrder
3  DoDDirective, DoDInstruction, DirectiveTypeMemorandum, Memorandum, OpmIssuance
4  NavyIssuance
5  MarineCorpsOrder, ServiceMessage (MARADMIN)

The relationships

  • derivesAuthorityFrom — transitive; the cited authority a document flows from.
  • inferredAuthorityFrom — a subproperty of the above, for links not stated in the text but inferred — so an inference is never mistaken for a citation.
  • references — cites without deriving authority.
  • amends / supersedes / clarifies — public-law-amends-statute, reissue-cancels-prior, follow-on-clarifies-prior.
  • partOf — a provision belongs to its issuance.
  • governs — a provision controls a named Decision.

The governs edge makes the lineage literal: 10 U.S.C. 701(h), the DoDI's 3.11.c, the DTM, and MARADMIN 051/23 all govern the same ParentalLeaveCapDecision — one rule traced from the Marine up to enacted law.

The spine — the identifier scheme

One path-style address per provision, used identically in the markup (@identifier) and the graph (@id):

/us/usc/10/701/h/1/A                  ← statute, subparagraph
/us/dod/dtm/2023/23-001/att3/p3/a/1   ← DTM, deep procedure
/us/usmc/maradmin/2023/051/p11/d      ← MARADMIN, the 84-day cap

Pattern: /jurisdiction / organization / series / number / …provision path. Because both layers use the same string, a citation in the markup and its node in the authority graph point at the exact same thing.

The verification model

VERIFIED means confirmed against the retrieved issuance text; UNVERIFIED means present but the source line is not yet located. It is required by the schema, propagates onto every citation, and is the project's signature: the data distinguishes confirmed policy from asserted policy on its face.

What ties it together — the validator

tools/validate.py keeps the layers consistent, which is what makes the scheme trustworthy at scale:

  1. Schema — every *.uslm.xml validates against the XSD.
  2. Well-formedness — every JSON / JSON-LD parses.
  3. Ontology — the Turtle parses.
  4. Conformance — every pol: type/predicate used in a graph is declared in the ontology.
  5. Referential integrity — every citation and every Provision node resolves to a real @identifier in the markup.

Why it is built this way

Keep structure and authority in separate layers so each can be reasoned about independently, but bind them with one identifier spine so nothing drifts. Make addressability and verification schema-mandatory so the data cannot be sloppy. Model numbering and authority as data — not formatting, not prose — so machines can traverse it. Get the scheme right once, and thousands of issuances can run through it and be validated. That is the thesis.
